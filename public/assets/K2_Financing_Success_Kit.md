# Chapter 1: Preparing for Financing Success

## Introduction

In commercial real estate and business financing, preparation is the single most powerful advantage a borrower can have. The difference between a deal that closes quickly with favorable terms and one that stalls, gets declined, or results in unfavorable conditions almost always comes down to **how well the borrower is prepared before they ever approach a lender**.

This chapter will walk you through every critical step of preparation — from understanding your borrower profile to assembling a complete, lender-ready loan package. Whether you're a first-time commercial borrower or an experienced investor expanding your portfolio, the strategies here will position you for success.

## 1.1 Why Preparation Matters

### The Prepared Borrower Advantage

Lenders evaluate hundreds of loan requests. The ones that stand out — and get the fastest approvals with the best terms — share common traits:

- **Complete documentation** submitted from the start
- **Clear deal narrative** that tells the story of the investment
- **Organized financial presentation** that makes the lender's job easy
- **Realistic expectations** about loan terms and structure
- **Professional communication** at every touchpoint

### The Cost of Being Unprepared

Unprepared borrowers face real consequences:

- **Wasted time**: Incomplete applications sit in queues while prepared borrowers get funded
- **Higher rates**: Lenders price in risk when packages are disorganized
- **Reduced leverage**: Missing documentation leads to lower loan-to-value ratios
- **Lost deals**: Sellers and partners won't wait for borrowers who can't perform
- **Reputation damage**: In a relationship-driven industry, being unprepared is remembered

## 1.2 Understanding Your Borrower Profile

Before approaching any lender, you need a clear picture of how you'll be evaluated. Every commercial lender assesses five key areas:

### Credit Profile

Your personal credit score matters, but it's only part of the picture. Lenders evaluate:

- **Personal FICO score**: Most require 680+ for conventional; 640+ for SBA programs
- **Credit history depth**: Length of established credit relationships
- **Derogatory marks**: Late payments, collections, bankruptcies, and their recency
- **Credit utilization**: Keep revolving balances below 30% of available limits
- **Recent inquiries**: Multiple hard pulls in a short period raise flags

**Action Step**: Pull your credit report from all three bureaus. Address any errors. If your score needs improvement, begin optimization at least 90 days before applying.

### Net Worth & Liquidity

Lenders want to know you have skin in the game and reserves for unexpected situations:

- **Minimum net worth**: Typically equal to or greater than the loan amount
- **Liquid reserves**: Usually 6–12 months of debt service payments
- **Down payment source**: Funds must be sourced and seasoned (typically 60–90 days in your account)
- **Personal Financial Statement (PFS)**: Must be current, accurate, and comprehensive

**Action Step**: Complete a Personal Financial Statement using a standard template. Include all assets at realistic fair market values and all liabilities. Update this document monthly.

### Real Estate Experience

Your track record matters more than most borrowers realize:

- **Years in real estate**: Longer track record = better pricing and terms
- **Number of properties**: Portfolio size demonstrates management capability
- **Property type experience**: Lenders prefer experience in the same asset class
- **Performance history**: Track record of successful exits and stabilized properties

**Action Step**: Create a "Real Estate Resume" — a professional one-page document listing your properties, purchase prices, current values, management approach, and outcomes.

### Cash Flow & Income

Lenders need to verify your ability to service debt:

- **Personal income**: W-2s, K-1s, tax returns (2–3 years)
- **Business income**: P&L statements, business tax returns
- **Property income**: Rent rolls, operating statements, NOI calculations
- **Global cash flow**: Combined across all properties and businesses

**Action Step**: Organize your last three years of personal and business tax returns. Prepare a global cash flow analysis showing all income sources and debt obligations.

### The Deal Itself

The property or business acquisition must make sense on its own merits:

- **Property condition**: Recent inspections, environmental reports
- **Market fundamentals**: Occupancy rates, rent comparables, demographic trends
- **Business plan**: What you'll do with the property (hold, renovate, reposition, etc.)
- **Exit strategy**: How you'll eventually repay or refinance the loan

## 1.3 Assembling Your Loan Package

A complete loan package is your first impression with any lender. Treat it as a professional presentation of both yourself and your deal.

### The Executive Summary

This is the single most important document in your package. A strong executive summary:

- Fits on **one page** (two pages maximum)
- Leads with the **loan request** (amount, purpose, property type)
- Highlights **borrower strengths** (experience, credit, liquidity)
- Summarizes the **deal** (property, location, financials)
- States the **value proposition** for the lender

### Personal Financial Documents

| Document | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Personal Financial Statement | Full asset/liability snapshot |
| 2–3 years personal tax returns | Income verification |
| Bank statements (3 months) | Liquidity and cash verification |
| Credit authorization form | Allows lender to pull credit report |
| Real estate schedule | List of all owned properties with values and debt |
| Resume/CV | Professional background and experience |

### Business Financial Documents

| Document | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Business tax returns (2–3 years) | Business income verification |
| Year-to-date P&L statement | Current performance snapshot |
| Balance sheet | Business assets and liabilities |
| Articles of incorporation/LLC docs | Entity structure verification |
| Business plan | Strategic direction and projections |

### Property-Specific Documents

| Document | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Property information (address, type, size) | Basic identification |
| Purchase contract or refinance details | Terms and timing |
| Rent roll (current) | Income verification for investment properties |
| Operating statements (2–3 years) | Historical performance |
| Photos and property condition report | Physical assessment |
| Environmental Phase I report | Environmental risk assessment |
| Appraisal (if available) | Value determination |
| Lease abstracts | Tenant quality and lease terms |

## 1.4 Creating Your Deal Narrative

Numbers tell the story, but every strong loan package includes a compelling narrative that connects the dots for the lender. Your deal narrative should answer:

1. **Who are you?** Experience, track record, financial strength
2. **What is the deal?** Property type, location, condition, price
3. **Why this deal?** Market opportunity, value-add potential, strategic fit
4. **How will it work?** Business plan, projections, management approach
5. **What's the ask?** Specific loan terms you're seeking
6. **What's the exit?** How you'll repay or refinance

### Writing Tips

- Use clear, professional language — not sales speak
- Lead with strengths, but don't hide weaknesses; address them proactively
- Include specific numbers: "25 years of CRE experience with $12M in portfolio value"
- Keep it concise: lenders are busy and appreciate efficiency
- Have someone knowledgeable review it before submission

## 1.5 Financial Analysis Fundamentals

### Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)

DSCR is the #1 metric lenders evaluate for income-producing properties:

$$DSCR = \frac{Net\ Operating\ Income}{Annual\ Debt\ Service}$$

- **1.25x or higher**: Strong — most lenders will be comfortable
- **1.20x–1.25x**: Acceptable for strong borrowers with other compensating factors
- **Below 1.20x**: Challenging — may need additional collateral or guarantees

### Net Operating Income (NOI)

$$NOI = Gross\ Rental\ Income - Vacancy\ Allowance - Operating\ Expenses$$

Key considerations:
- Use **actual** vacancy rates, not market averages
- Include **all** operating expenses: property taxes, insurance, management, maintenance, utilities, reserves
- **Do not** include debt service, depreciation, or income taxes in NOI

### Loan-to-Value Ratio (LTV)

$$LTV = \frac{Loan\ Amount}{Appraised\ Value} \times 100$$

Typical maximum LTV ratios:
- **Conventional**: 65–75%
- **SBA 504**: Up to 90%
- **Bridge loans**: 65–70%
- **Construction**: 60–75% (of completed value)

## 1.6 Preparation Checklist

Before approaching any lender, confirm:

- [ ] Personal Financial Statement is complete and current
- [ ] 2–3 years of personal tax returns organized and ready
- [ ] 3 months of bank statements gathered
- [ ] Credit report reviewed and any issues addressed
- [ ] Real estate schedule prepared with all properties listed
- [ ] Business financials organized (if applicable)
- [ ] Executive Summary drafted and reviewed
- [ ] Deal narrative written with supporting data
- [ ] DSCR calculated with realistic assumptions
- [ ] Property documents collected
- [ ] Understanding of what loan program you're targeting

# Chapter 2: Understanding and Selecting the Loan Program

## Introduction

One of the most common mistakes borrowers make is applying for the wrong loan program. Each program has specific eligibility requirements, advantages, and limitations. Choosing the right one can save you tens of thousands of dollars over the life of your loan and dramatically improve your chances of approval.

## 2.1 Conventional Commercial Mortgages

### Overview

Traditional bank loans for commercial properties, typically from banks, credit unions, and savings institutions.

### Key Characteristics

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Loan amounts | $250K – $25M+ |
| LTV | 65–75% |
| Term | 5–10 years (with 20–25 year amortization) |
| Interest rates | Fixed or adjustable; typically Prime + margin |
| DSCR requirement | 1.20x – 1.35x |
| Recourse | Usually full recourse for smaller loans |
| Prepayment | May have yield maintenance or defeasance |
| Timing | 45–90 days typical closing |

### Best For

- Experienced borrowers with strong credit (700+)
- Stabilized, income-producing properties
- Borrowers with 25–35% down payment
- Properties in strong markets with proven cash flow

### Watch Out For

- Balloon payments at term end (refinance risk)
- Personal guarantee requirements
- Annual financial reporting requirements
- Potential covenant triggers

## 2.2 SBA 504 Loans

### Overview

Government-backed program designed for owner-occupied commercial properties and major business equipment. The loan is structured as a partnership between a bank (50%), a Certified Development Company (40%), and the borrower (10%).

### Key Characteristics

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Loan amounts | $500K – $5.5M (CDC portion up to $5.5M) |
| LTV | Up to 90% |
| Term | 10, 20, or 25 years |
| Interest rates | Below-market fixed rate (CDC portion pegged to treasury) |
| DSCR requirement | 1.15x – 1.25x |
| Occupancy | Borrower must occupy 51%+ of property |
| Prepayment | First 10 years on 20-year debenture |

### Best For

- Owner-occupied properties (office, retail, warehouse, medical, etc.)
- Business owners who want maximum leverage (only 10% down)
- Long-term holds with fixed-rate security
- Job creation or retention scenarios

### Watch Out For

- Must occupy 51%+ of the property
- Cannot be used for investment-only properties
- Additional fees from CDC and SBA
- Longer processing times (60–120 days)
- Must demonstrate public benefit (job creation)

## 2.3 SBA 7(a) Loans

### Overview

The SBA's flagship program, more flexible than 504 but typically used for smaller commercial transactions and working capital needs.

### Key Characteristics

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Loan amounts | Up to $5M |
| LTV | Up to 90% for real estate |
| Term | 25 years (real estate), 10 years (equipment), 7 years (working capital) |
| Interest rates | Variable (Prime + 2.25–2.75%) or fixed options |
| DSCR requirement | 1.15x – 1.25x |
| Guarantee fee | 2–3.75% (one-time, can be financed) |

### Best For

- Mixed-use transactions (real estate + working capital + equipment)
- Business acquisitions with real estate
- Startup or early-stage businesses with strong owners
- Borrowers who need flexibility in use of funds

### Watch Out For

- Variable rate risk on most 7(a) loans
- Guarantee fee adds to cost
- Detailed business plan required
- SBA documentation requirements are extensive
- Personal guarantee always required

## 2.4 DSCR-Only Loans (Investor Loans)

### Overview

Non-QM loans that qualify based on the property's cash flow rather than the borrower's personal income. Popular with real estate investors.

### Key Characteristics

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Loan amounts | $100K – $5M |
| LTV | 65–80% |
| Term | 30-year fixed or adjustable |
| Interest rates | Higher than conventional (typically 1–2% premium) |
| DSCR requirement | 1.0x – 1.25x minimum |
| Documentation | Minimal borrower docs; property income focused |
| Recourse | Available as non-recourse in some cases |

### Best For

- Self-employed investors with complex tax returns
- Investors who want speed and simplicity
- Portfolio builders acquiring multiple properties
- Borrowers with strong properties but weaker personal financials

### Watch Out For

- Higher interest rates
- Lower maximum LTV
- Higher reserves requirements
- Limited to investment properties only
- Rate premiums for lower DSCR ratios

## 2.5 Bridge Loans

### Overview

Short-term financing for transitional situations: acquisitions needing renovation, properties being stabilized, or time-sensitive opportunities.

### Key Characteristics

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Loan amounts | $500K – $50M+ |
| LTV | 65–75% (as-is value) |
| Term | 12–36 months (with extension options) |
| Interest rates | 8–14% (interest-only) |
| Points | 1–3 origination points |
| Speed | 2–4 week closings possible |

### Best For

- Value-add acquisitions requiring renovation
- Properties with below-market occupancy being stabilized
- Time-sensitive acquisition opportunities
- Borrowers planning to refinance into permanent financing

### Watch Out For

- Very high cost of capital
- Short term creates refinance pressure
- Extension fees can be significant
- Default consequences are severe

## 2.6 Construction Loans

### Overview

Financing for ground-up development or major renovation projects.

### Key Characteristics

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Loan amounts | $1M – $100M+ |
| LTC (Loan-to-Cost) | 60–80% |
| Term | 12–36 months + stabilization period |
| Interest rates | Variable; higher than permanent financing |
| Draw schedule | Funds released as construction milestones are met |
| Guarantor | Strong personal guarantees required |

### Best For

- Ground-up development projects
- Major rebuilds or repositioning
- Experienced developers with track record

### Watch Out For

- Highest risk loan type — lenders are very selective
- Cost overrun risk is borrower's responsibility
- Interest reserve required (pre-funded)
- Requires detailed budget, timeline, and contractor documentation
- Personal guarantee is non-negotiable

## 2.7 How to Choose the Right Program

### Decision Framework

Ask yourself these questions:

1. **Will I occupy the property?**
   - Yes → SBA 504 or 7(a) (maximize leverage)
   - No → Conventional, DSCR, or bridge

2. **What is my timeline?**
   - Need speed → Bridge or DSCR
   - Can wait for best terms → Conventional or SBA

3. **How strong are my personal financials?**
   - Strong → Conventional (best rates)
   - Complex → DSCR (property-income focus)

4. **What is the property condition?**
   - Stabilized → Conventional or SBA
   - Needs work → Bridge or construction

5. **How long will I hold the property?**
   - Long-term → Fixed-rate conventional or SBA 504
   - Short-term → Bridge or adjustable rate

### Loan Comparison Worksheet

Use the companion K2 Loan Comparison Spreadsheet to organize and compare offers side-by-side. Key columns include:

- Lender name and contact
- Loan program type
- Interest rate and type (fixed/variable)
- Term and amortization
- LTV and loan amount
- Origination fees
- Prepayment penalty terms
- Recourse requirements
- Closing costs estimate
- Special conditions or covenants

# Chapter 3: Finding the Right Lender

## Introduction

The lender you choose matters as much as the loan program. A great lender match means smoother processing, better communication, and often better terms. A poor match means wasted time, frustration, and potentially a declined application — even when your deal is solid.

This chapter shows you how to identify, evaluate, and approach the right lenders for your specific deal.

## 3.1 Types of Lenders

### Commercial Banks

**Best for**: Conventional loans, SBA programs, established borrower relationships

- Full-service lending with deposit relationships
- Relationship-driven pricing (existing customers get better terms)
- Generally conservative underwriting
- Slower but thorough process

### Credit Unions

**Best for**: Smaller commercial loans, community-focused deals

- Often more flexible than large banks
- Member-focused (may offer slightly better rates)
- Typically smaller loan limits
- More personalized service

### Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs)

**Best for**: Underserved markets, community impact projects

- Mission-driven lending
- More flexible credit requirements
- Often provide technical assistance
- May offer below-market rates for qualifying projects

### Private Lenders / Hard Money

**Best for**: Bridge loans, fix-and-flip, time-sensitive deals

- Fastest closing (days to weeks)
- Asset-based underwriting (property matters more than borrower)
- Highest rates (8–15%+)
- Short terms (6–36 months)

### Life Insurance Companies

**Best for**: Large, stabilized commercial properties

- Lowest rates in the market
- Non-recourse options available
- Long terms (10–30 years)
- Very selective (Class A properties, strong markets only)
- High minimum loan amounts ($5M+)

### CMBS Lenders

**Best for**: Mid-size to large commercial properties that need non-recourse

- Loans securitized and sold to investors
- Non-recourse standard
- Fixed rates with longer terms
- More rigid underwriting (less relationship flexibility)
- Minimum typically $2M+

### Debt Funds

**Best for**: Bridge, construction, value-add deals

- More flexible than traditional lenders
- Higher leverage available
- Speed of closing
- Higher cost than conventional

## 3.2 Hint: It May Not Be Your Local Bank

One of the biggest mistakes borrowers make is assuming their local bank — where they have their checking account — is the best lender for their commercial deal. In reality:

- **Your bank may not do commercial lending** at the level you need
- **Portfolio lenders** in other markets may specialize in your asset type
- **National lenders** may offer better programs for your specific situation
- **SBA lenders**, **CDFIs**, and **credit unions** may provide options your bank doesn't offer

### How to Cast a Wider Net

1. **Use K2's Preferred Lender Network**: Pre-vetted lenders who specialize in deals like yours
2. **Search the SBA Lender Match tool**: Free matching service at sba.gov
3. **Attend local CRE networking events**: Meet lenders face-to-face
4. **Ask other investors**: Referrals from experienced borrowers are invaluable
5. **Work with a broker** (like K2): Access to relationships you'd never find on your own

## 3.3 Evaluating Lender Fit

Before submitting your package, evaluate lenders on these criteria:

### Product Match

- Does this lender offer the specific loan program you need?
- Do they lend on your property type and in your market?
- Does your deal size fit their lending range?
- Are they currently active in your deal's asset class?

### Process Quality

- How long does their typical closing take?
- What is their application and documentation process?
- Will you have a dedicated point of contact?
- What are their communication standards?

### Cost Structure

- What is their current rate range?
- What origination fees do they charge?
- Are there application or processing fees?
- What are the prepayment penalty terms?
- What third-party costs do they require (appraisal, environmental, legal)?

### Relationship Potential

- Is this a one-time transaction or potential long-term relationship?
- Will they consider future deals based on this relationship?
- Do they offer other services (deposit accounts, treasury management)?

## 3.4 Approaching Lenders Like a Professional

### The First Contact Framework

Your initial outreach should be concise, professional, and demonstrate that you've done your homework:

**Phone Script** (30–45 seconds):

> "Hi, my name is [Name] and I'm looking for financing on a [property type] in [location]. It's a [purchase/refinance] at approximately [price/loan amount]. I have [X years] of CRE experience and [brief strength — e.g., strong credit, 30% equity, stabilized property]. Would this fit your lending criteria? I have a complete package ready to send."

**Email Template**:

> Subject: [Property Type] Financing Request — [City, State]
>
> Dear [Lender Name],
>
> I am writing to inquire about financing for a [property type] located at [address]. This is a [purchase/refinance] transaction with the following highlights:
>
> - **Loan Request**: [Amount]
> - **Property Type**: [Type, size]
> - **Borrower Experience**: [Brief summary]
> - **Key Strength**: [Highlight one]
>
> I have a complete loan package including executive summary, financial statements, and property documentation ready for immediate review.
>
> Would this request fit your current lending criteria? I'd welcome a brief call to discuss.
>
> Best regards,
> [Your Name]

### Follow-Up Cadence

- **Day 1**: Initial outreach (phone or email)
- **Day 3**: Follow up if no response (try the other channel)
- **Day 7**: Second follow-up with a brief note
- **Day 14**: Final attempt; if still no response, move to other lenders

### What Not to Do

- Don't mass-blast applications to dozens of lenders simultaneously
- Don't misrepresent any facts about yourself or the deal
- Don't be combative or overly aggressive in negotiations
- Don't apply without understanding the lender's minimum requirements
- Don't send incomplete packages and promise to "follow up with the rest later"

## 3.5 Managing Multiple Lender Conversations

When pursuing the best terms, you'll likely speak with 3–5 lenders simultaneously. Stay organized:

- **Use the K2 Loan Comparison Spreadsheet** to track each conversation
- **Log every interaction**: Date, contact person, key points discussed
- **Set calendar reminders** for follow-ups
- **Be transparent**: You can (and should) mention you're evaluating options
- **Compare apples to apples**: Ensure you're comparing same-structure quotes

# Chapter 4: Navigating Underwriting and Closing Your Deal

## Introduction

You've prepared your package, selected the right loan program, found a lender, and received initial interest. Now comes the most critical phase: underwriting and closing. This is where deals live or die, and where prepared borrowers separate themselves from the pack.

## 4.1 Understanding the Underwriting Process

### What Underwriters Evaluate

Underwriting is a systematic risk assessment. The underwriter will verify and analyze:

1. **Borrower creditworthiness**: Credit score verification, background checks, litigation search
2. **Property value**: Appraisal review, comparable analysis, market assessment
3. **Cash flow sustainability**: Income verification, rent roll analysis, expense review
4. **Collateral adequacy**: Property condition, environmental risk, insurance requirements
5. **Relationship history**: Existing accounts, payment track record, banking relationship

### The Five C's of Credit

Every underwriter evaluates these, whether they state them or not:

| C | What They're Assessing |
|---|----------------------|
| **Character** | Your track record, reputation, and trustworthiness |
| **Capacity** | Ability to repay based on cash flow and income |
| **Capital** | Your personal investment (skin in the game) |
| **Conditions** | Market conditions, economic outlook, industry trends |
| **Collateral** | Property or assets securing the loan |

## 4.2 Common Underwriting Red Flags

Understanding what triggers concerns helps you address them proactively:

### Borrower Red Flags

- Declining personal income over recent years
- High personal debt-to-income ratio
- Recent credit events (late payments, collections, judgments)
- Insufficient liquidity (less than 6 months reserves)
- Tax liens or unresolved IRS issues
- Multiple entities making ownership structure unclear

### Property Red Flags

- Below-market occupancy with no clear stabilization plan
- Environmental contamination (Phase I or II issues)
- Deferred maintenance or structural concerns
- Concentration risk (single tenant = single point of failure)
- Lease rollover risk (multiple leases expiring simultaneously)
- Market weakness (declining rents, rising vacancy in the area)

### Deal Structure Red Flags

- Purchase price above appraised value
- Unrealistic rent projections or occupancy assumptions
- Insufficient down payment or equity
- Complex entity structures with no clear benefit
- Seller financing that creates subordinate debt without disclosure

## 4.3 Responding to Underwriting Requests

Underwriters will send a list of conditions and additional documentation requests. How you respond matters:

### Best Practices

- **Respond within 24–48 hours**: Speed signals professionalism
- **Provide exactly what's requested**: Don't under-deliver or over-send
- **Organize documents clearly**: Name files logically, use folders
- **Address concerns directly**: If there's a weak point, explain it don't ignore it
- **Keep communication professional**: Email is the paper trail; be thoughtful

### Common Conditions to Expect

- Updated bank statements (within 30 days)
- Verification of funds for closing
- Additional property documentation
- Updated rent roll within 30 days of closing
- Evidence of hazard and liability insurance
- Borrower entity formation documents
- Full environmental Phase I report
- Title commitment review

## 4.4 The Appraisal Process

### Understanding Approaches to Value

Commercial appraisals typically use three approaches:

#### Income Approach (Most Weighted for Investment Properties)

$$Value = \frac{Net\ Operating\ Income}{Capitalization\ Rate}$$

- Based on the property's actual and projected income
- Uses current market cap rates for comparison
- Most relevant for income-producing properties

#### Sales Comparison Approach

- Analyzes recent comparable property sales
- Adjustments for differences in size, condition, location, and amenities
- Most relevant for owner-occupied and smaller properties

#### Cost Approach

- Estimates replacement cost of the building plus land value
- Less relevant for income properties
- Used for special-purpose or newer buildings

### When the Appraisal Comes in Low

If the appraised value is below the purchase price or your expectations:

1. **Review the report carefully**: Look for errors in comparable selections, income figures, or expense assumptions
2. **Request a reconsideration of value**: Provide additional comparables or correct factual errors
3. **Negotiate with the seller**: A low appraisal can be a negotiating tool
4. **Bring additional equity**: Cover the gap between appraised value and loan amount
5. **Consider a different lender**: Another lender may use a different appraiser

## 4.5 Title and Legal Review

### Title Insurance

Both a lender's title policy and an owner's title policy are standard in commercial transactions:

- **Lender's policy**: Required by the lender; protects their interest
- **Owner's policy**: Protects your ownership interest; highly recommended
- **Title search**: Identifies liens, encumbrances, easements, and other issues
- **Survey**: Confirms property boundaries and identifies encroachments

### Entity and Legal Documentation

Before closing, ensure:

- Operating Agreement or Bylaws are current and authorize the transaction
- All required member/partner consents are documented
- Good standing certificates are obtained from the Secretary of State
- Tax clearance certificates are obtained (if required by your state)
- Any required subordination agreements are in place

## 4.6 Closing Process

### Pre-Closing Checklist

In the days before closing:

- [ ] Review final HUD-1/Closing Disclosure line by line
- [ ] Confirm wire transfer instructions directly with the title company
- [ ] Verify insurance policies are bound and certificates submitted to lender
- [ ] Confirm all conditions in the commitment letter have been satisfied
- [ ] Review loan documents received from lender's counsel
- [ ] Prepare certified checks for any amounts not wired
- [ ] Bring government-issued photo ID to closing
- [ ] Have keys, access codes, and property management contacts ready

### At the Closing Table

- **Take your time reviewing documents**: Don't rush through signatures
- **Ask questions**: If something doesn't match what you expected, stop and clarify
- **Verify numbers**: Compare the closing disclosure to the commitment letter
- **Keep copies**: Get copies of everything you sign

### Post-Closing Actions

- Confirm recording of documents (deed, mortgage/deed of trust)
- Set up automatic loan payments
- Notify tenants of ownership change (if applicable)
- Update your insurance to reflect the new financing
- Begin your property management plan
- File any required entity documents

## 4.7 Managing Your Loan Long-Term

### Annual Requirements

Most commercial loans require ongoing compliance:

- **Annual financial statements**: Personal and property-level
- **Annual operating statements**: Actual income and expenses
- **Rent roll updates**: Current tenant information
- **Insurance renewal**: Evidence of continued coverage
- **Tax payment confirmation**: Proof property taxes are current

### Refinancing Strategy

Start planning your refinance or exit 12–18 months before loan maturity:

- Track your property's performance and NOI improvement
- Monitor interest rate trends
- Begin lender conversations early
- Update your loan package 6 months before maturity
- Complete appraisal process 3–4 months before maturity

### Building Lender Relationships

The best financing terms come from repeated successful interactions:

- **Communicate proactively**: Don't wait for lenders to chase you for documents
- **Report good news**: Share positive property performance updates
- **Refer other borrowers**: Lenders appreciate business introductions
- **Be transparent about challenges**: Address issues before they become problems
- **Maintain your banking relationship**: Deposit accounts matter

| Metric | Formula |

| DSCR | NOI ÷ Annual Debt Service |
